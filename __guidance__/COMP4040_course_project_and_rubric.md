# 1. Course Project Purpose

The final project is designed to help students apply data mining and big data analytics techniques to real-world or research-oriented problems. The project should help students experience the process of:

- Identifying a meaningful data-driven problem.
- Developing and refining a project idea over time.
- Acquiring relevant technical knowledge and tools.
- Setting up a valid data mining task.
- Testing and evaluating existing solutions.
- Possibly proposing improved or novel solutions.
- Communicating technical findings through proposals, milestones, presentations, and a final report.

The project can be either:

- **Application-oriented**, where the goal is to solve a real-world task involving data analysis.
- **Research-oriented**, where the goal is to investigate or develop a method, algorithm, or analysis that may contribute to a broader research direction.

The course encourages students to choose ambitious and interesting topics, as long as the topic involves data and data-analysis tasks.

# 3. General Evaluation Criteria

The project presentation and final report make up the majority of the project grade. Projects are evaluated according to three major dimensions:

## 3.1 Technical Quality

Technical quality asks whether the project is technically sound, meaningful, and sufficiently developed.

A strong project should demonstrate that:

- The technical material makes sense.
- The methods tried are reasonable for the problem.
- The proposed algorithms, applications, or analyses are clever and interesting.
- The authors provide novel insight about the problem, algorithms, or data.
- The project has sufficient breadth and depth for the team size.
- The experiments are appropriate and support the claims made in the report or presentation.
- The implementation is credible and clearly described.

For a three-person team, the expected project scope is larger than for a two-person team.

## 3.2 Originality

Originality evaluates whether the team added something of its own rather than simply reproducing existing work.

A strong project should include at least one of the following:

- Original data processing.
- Original analysis.
- A modified or improved method.
- A new application of existing methods.
- A new comparison or evaluation setting.
- A meaningful insight about the dataset, problem, or algorithm.

The project should avoid being a mirror image of an existing paper, GitHub repository, or online project without new work.

## 3.3 Communication

Communication evaluates how clearly and effectively the team explains the project.

A strong project should:

- Clearly explain the problem context.
- Clearly state the motivation and importance of the problem.
- Explain the methods rigorously but understandably.
- Present results in a clear and convincing way.
- Balance clarity with technical rigor.
- Include meaningful discussion of what was learned from developing the method.
- Compare the work against existing approaches.

# 4. Deliverables and Rubric Details

## 4.1 Deliverable 1: Preliminary Proposal

### Purpose

The preliminary proposal is intended to make the team choose a project topic early and receive feedback from the teaching team.

### Format

The proposal should be submitted as a PDF document. It should include:

- Project title.
- Project category.
- Full names of all team members.
- VinUni emails of all team members.
- A description of the planned project, up to 1 page.

### Required Content

The preliminary proposal should include the following sections or information:

#### Motivation

Explain:

- What problem the team is tackling.
- Whether the project is an application or theoretical result.
- Why the problem is important.
- The potential impact of solving the problem.
- The main challenges in solving the problem.

#### Method

Explain:

- What data mining techniques the team plans to apply.
- Whether the team plans to improve any existing techniques.
- Why the chosen methods are appropriate for the problem.

#### Intended Experiments

Explain:

- Whether the team already has data or plans to obtain new data.
- What experiments the team plans to run.
- How the team plans to evaluate the method.

Optional but useful additions:

- A pointer to one relevant dataset.
- A pointer to one example of prior research on the topic.

### Grading Criteria

This deliverable is mainly graded on whether:

- The proposal follows the required instructions.
- The team has chosen a project topic.
- The project seems reasonably planned.
- The proposed direction appears feasible enough to receive early feedback.

This deliverable is not primarily judged on final technical success. It is mainly a checkpoint for early planning.

## 4.2 Deliverable 2: Proposal Presentation

### Purpose

The proposal presentation helps the team consolidate the project idea, perform a preliminary literature review, and receive feedback from instructors and classmates.

### Format

- Presentation duration: strictly 7 minutes.
- Q&A duration: 3 minutes.

### Required Components

The presentation should clearly explain:

#### Problem

- What problem the team is tackling.
- Why the problem matters.
- What challenges exist in solving the problem.

#### Existing Approaches and Limitations

- What existing methods or systems already attempt to solve the problem.
- What limitations these existing approaches have.
- Why there is still room for improvement or further analysis.

#### Proposed Solution

- What the team plans to improve compared with existing approaches.
- Why the team believes the proposed solution will work.
- What the main technical idea is.

#### Data

- Where the data will come from.
- Whether the data is public, private, or self-collected.
- Whether data collection or access may create risks.

#### Experiments

- What experiments the team plans to run.
- How the method will be evaluated.
- What metrics, baselines, or comparisons may be used.

#### Contributions

The presentation must include a slide describing what each team member worked on and contributed to the project.

### Grading Criteria

The proposal presentation is judged based on the clarity of explanation of the required components above.

A strong presentation should:

- Make the problem easy to understand.
- Explain why the problem is important and challenging.
- Show awareness of existing approaches.
- Clearly identify limitations in previous work.
- Present a feasible solution direction.
- Explain the data source and experimental plan.
- Show fair and concrete team contribution.

## 4.3 Deliverable 3: Final Proposal

### Purpose

The final proposal is the evolved version of the preliminary proposal. By this stage, the team should have a clearer understanding of the problem, related work, methodology, data, baselines, and evaluation plan.

### Format

- PDF document.
- Maximum length: 3 pages.
- Must include project title, project category, full names, and VinUni emails of all team members.

### Required Sections

#### Introduction

The introduction should clearly describe:

- The problem.
- The challenges of the problem.
- Limitations of existing approaches.
- The proposed approach.
- The contributions of the project.
- The novelty or originality of the work.
- The expected impact of the work.

The document suggests using strong Data Mining or Machine Learning papers as references for how to structure the introduction.

#### Related Work

This section should thoroughly discuss relevant prior work.

Important distinction:

- The introduction should only briefly mention related work or give an overview of the problem domain.
- The related work section should provide a deeper discussion of relevant methods, systems, datasets, and prior findings.

#### Method

The method section should include:

##### Problem Statement

Provide a formal statement of the problem.

This may include:

- Input data.
- Output prediction, pattern, ranking, cluster, or analysis target.
- Objective of the task.
- Constraints or assumptions.

##### Approach

Describe the proposed solution and explain how it addresses the challenges mentioned earlier.

This should include:

- Main algorithm or pipeline.
- Data processing steps.
- Feature engineering or representation learning.
- Model or analysis method.
- Any proposed modification or improvement.

#### Experiments

The experiment section should clearly describe what experiments will be performed.

It should include:

##### Datasets

- What datasets will be used.
- Where the datasets come from.
- Summary statistics of the datasets.

##### Metrics

- What metrics will be used to evaluate performance.
- Why those metrics are appropriate.

##### Baselines

- What baseline methods will be used.
- Why these baselines are reasonable comparisons.

##### Implementation Details

- How the baselines and proposed method will be implemented.
- Important tools, libraries, or frameworks.
- Any important training or evaluation settings.

##### Preliminary Results

- Any early results already obtained.
- Initial findings, even if incomplete.
- Early evidence that the project is feasible.

### Writing Expectation

The intended audience is scientific readers who may already have some knowledge of data mining techniques and the problem domain.

Therefore:

- Do not spend too much space explaining basic concepts.
- Do not explain technical methods in only one or two vague sentences.
- Aim for a balance between concise explanation and technical clarity.

### Grading Criteria

The final proposal is judged based on the clarity of the report.

A strong final proposal should:

- Be well-structured.
- Clearly state the problem and motivation.
- Explain related work meaningfully.
- Present a formal problem statement.
- Describe the proposed method clearly.
- Include datasets, metrics, baselines, implementation details, and preliminary results.
- Demonstrate that the project is feasible and technically meaningful.

The final project writeup is expected to remain on the same topic as the final proposal.

- Include an extra page describing in detail what has been added since the last report.

The milestone should update:

- Introduction.
- Related Work.
- Method.
- Experiments.
- Dataset description.
- Metrics.
- Baselines.
- Implementation details.
- Preliminary or updated results.
- Team contributions.

The extra page should clearly explain what has changed or been added since the final proposal.

### Grading Criteria

Milestone 1 is mostly intended for feedback and progress tracking.

The team should do well if:

- The milestone follows the instructions.
- The team shows reasonable progress.
- The team has tested assumptions that might prevent project completion.
- The report clearly explains new work since the previous submission.
- The contribution section shows that team members are carrying a fair share of the work.

Important hidden expectation:

The phrase “tested assumptions that might prevent your team from completing the project” means the team should already have checked major feasibility risks, such as:

- Whether the dataset is accessible.
- Whether labels are available or can be generated.
- Whether the model can run on available compute.
- Whether the evaluation metric is computable.
- Whether baselines can be implemented.
- Whether the task is too broad or too narrow.

# 7. Rules on Collaboration and Reuse

## 7.1 Collaboration Between Teams

Teams are not allowed to collaborate with each other.

If two teams independently choose the same project topic, that is acceptable as long as they do not coordinate in a way that biases or duplicates their work.

## 7.2 Combining with Another Class

Combining a COMP4040 project with another class is generally discouraged but may be allowed case by case.

Conditions:

- The team must inform the teaching team immediately.
- Permission must be obtained before or by the final project proposal stage.
- Requests after the final project proposal will not be granted.
- The project must still satisfy all COMP4040 requirements.
- The same identical project cannot be submitted for both classes.
- Shared infrastructure, codebase, or datasets may be allowed.
- The milestone and final report must clearly indicate which parts were done for COMP4040 and which parts were done for the other class.
- The final report from the other class must also be submitted if the project is shared.

## 7.3 Ongoing Research

The project must be new work.

Students cannot simply reuse existing work or research as the class project. However, the project may depend on existing research if:

- The class project is new work.
- The project is sufficiently self-contained.
- The report clearly states what was done before and what was done for COMP4040.

The class project may later be used for publications.

## 7.4 External Collaborators or Mentors

If the project involves collaborators, research groups, industry mentors, or external teams, the team must clearly state the work done by COMP4040-enrolled students.

Potential intellectual property implications should be discussed with the teaching team.

# 9. Practical Checklist for a High-Scoring Project

- The problem is relevant to data mining, big data analytics, or machine learning.

# 11. Key Takeaway

The COMP4040 final project is not graded only on whether the model achieves a high score. The rubric strongly rewards a complete data mining project process: clear problem formulation, meaningful technical work, originality, careful experiments, comparison with prior work, strong communication, and fair team contribution.

A high-scoring project should look like a compact scientific project rather than a simple coding assignment.
